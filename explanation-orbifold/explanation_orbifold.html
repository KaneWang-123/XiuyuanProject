1. What is an Orbifold? The Core Idea

You are already familiar with quotient spaces from GR and topology. The simplest example is the torus: take $\mathbb{R}^n$ and identify points related by a lattice $\Lambda$,

$$T^n = \mathbb{R}^n / \Lambda.$$

The lattice acts freely — no point of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is fixed by any non-trivial lattice element. The result is a perfectly smooth manifold.

An orbifold is what you get when you further quotient by a discrete (finite) group $G$ that does not act freely — some points are left fixed. Formally:

$$\mathcal{O} = M / G,$$

where $M$ is a smooth manifold and $G$ is a finite group acting on it by isometries. The non-free action means there exist fixed points $p \in M$ with $g \cdot p = p$ for some $g \neq e$. At those points the quotient space is locally a cone — it is singular as a manifold, but perfectly well-defined as a topological (and even metric) space.

Orbifold vs. manifold: A manifold is locally homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$. An orbifold is locally homeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n / \Gamma$, where $\Gamma$ is a finite group (the local isotropy group, possibly trivial). At generic points $\Gamma = \{e\}$ and the orbifold looks like a manifold. At fixed points $\Gamma \neq \{e\}$ and there is a genuine singularity.

In physics, orbifolds are used as compactification spaces because they are exactly solvable as string backgrounds (unlike generic Calabi-Yau manifolds), yet they break supersymmetry in controlled ways and can give chiral spectra.

2. The Simplest Example: $\mathbb{R}^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$ and $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$

2.1   $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}_2$: the half-line

Consider the real line $\mathbb{R}$ with the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ action $y \mapsto -y$. Two points $y$ and $-y$ are identified. The quotient is the half-line $[0,\infty)$. The unique fixed point is $y = 0$ — it maps to itself. Near $y = 0$ the space looks like two half-lines glued at their common endpoint: locally it is a "half-plane" or, in 1D, just a boundary.

2.2   $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$: the interval

More useful is the circle $S^1$, with coordinate $y \sim y + 2\pi R$, quotiented by $y \mapsto -y$. (Equivalently, $y \sim y + 2\pi R$ and $y \sim -y$.) The fundamental domain is $y \in [0, \pi R]$. There are two fixed points:

$$y^* = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad y^* = \pi R.$$

At $y=0$: the identification $y \to -y$ maps $y \to 0$ for $y$ near 0, so $y=0$ is fixed. At $y = \pi R$: $-(\pi R) \equiv 2\pi R - \pi R = \pi R$ (after the periodicity $y \sim y+2\pi R$), so $\pi R$ is also fixed. The quotient $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$ is topologically an interval $[0,\pi R]$, with each endpoint being an orbifold fixed point.

The circle $S^1$ quotiented by $y\mapsto -y$ gives an interval. The two endpoints are orbifold fixed points.

3. Geometry at Fixed Points: Conical Singularities

To see why fixed points are singular, consider $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $\mathbb{Z}_2: (x,y) \mapsto (-x,-y)$ (equivalently, $r e^{i\theta} \mapsto r e^{i(\theta+\pi)}$ in polar coordinates, or $\theta \sim \theta + \pi$). The quotient $\mathbb{R}^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ has angular coordinate $\theta \in [0,\pi)$ instead of $[0, 2\pi)$: it is a cone with half the opening angle.

The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ quotient halves the angular range, producing a cone with deficit angle $\pi$.

In GR language, the deficit angle is $\Delta\phi = 2\pi - \pi = \pi$. The curvature is everywhere zero except at the tip, where it is a delta function:

$$R = 2\pi(1 - 1/2)\,\delta^{(2)}(r) = \pi\,\delta^{(2)}(r).$$

More generally, for a $\mathbb{Z}_N$ quotient with action $\theta \mapsto \theta + 2\pi/N$, the deficit angle is $2\pi(1-1/N)$ and the cone has opening angle $2\pi/N$.

Physical interpretation: The orbifold fixed point is a conical singularity — a genuine curvature singularity from the GR perspective. In field theory, this means boundary conditions must be imposed there. In string theory, this singularity is resolved by the twisted sector (see §9).

For $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$, there are four fixed points (see §4). Each looks like $\mathbb{R}^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ locally — a cone of deficit angle $\pi$. The Euler characteristic receives contributions from these four conical singularities.

4. The Torus Orbifold $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$: Fixed Points and Fundamental Domain

This is the orbifold used repeatedly in arXiv:0806.4748. Start with the torus $T^2$ with coordinates $(y_1, y_2) \sim (y_1+1, y_2) \sim (y_1, y_2+1)$ (unit-cell convention). The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ acts as

$$\mathbb{Z}_2:\quad (y_1, y_2) \mapsto (-y_1, -y_2).$$

This is a rotation by $\pi$ (the inversion). It is an isometry of $T^2$. The fixed points are those satisfying $(y_1, y_2) \equiv (-y_1, -y_2) \pmod{\mathbb{Z}^2}$, i.e., $2y_i \in \mathbb{Z}$. There are exactly four:

$$(y_1, y_2) \in \left\{(0,0),\;\left(\tfrac{1}{2},0\right),\;\left(0,\tfrac{1}{2}\right),\;\left(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}\right)\right\}.$$

Unit cell of $T^2$. The four red dots are the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ fixed points. Each is a cone tip in the quotient $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$.

The quotient $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ is topologically a sphere $S^2$ — but with four conical singularities of deficit angle $\pi$ at the four fixed points. This is a special case of a "pillow" geometry: you can visualize it as folding the square torus in half diagonally.

Fixed point counting for $T^{2n}/\mathbb{Z}_2$: On $T^{2n}$ with the inversion $(y_1,\ldots,y_{2n}) \mapsto -(y_1,\ldots,y_{2n})$, there are $2^{2n}$ fixed points (each coordinate independently takes value $0$ or $\tfrac{1}{2}$). For $T^6/\mathbb{Z}_2$: $2^6 = 64$ fixed points.

5. Fields on an Orbifold: The Projection Condition

When you place a quantum field on an orbifold $M/G$, you do not simply restrict its domain. Instead, you keep the field on the covering space $M$ but impose that it transforms in a representation of $G$:

$$\phi(g \cdot y) = \rho(g)\,\phi(y) \quad \forall\, g \in G,$$

where $\rho: G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ is a representation of $G$ on the field's internal space $V$. This is the orbifold projection condition.

For $G = \mathbb{Z}_2$ with generator $\theta$ (so $\theta^2 = 1$), only two representations exist: the trivial representation $\rho(\theta) = +1$ and the sign representation $\rho(\theta) = -1$. Accordingly, fields must be either even or odd:

$$\phi(-y) = +\phi(y) \quad (\text{even, or ``$\mathbb{Z}_2$-parity $+$''}),$$ $$\phi(-y) = -\phi(y) \quad (\text{odd, or ``$\mathbb{Z}_2$-parity $-$''}).$$

Different fields (or different components of the same field) can be assigned different parities. This is a choice — it is data that specifies the orbifold model. The key consequences are:

  • Even fields have non-vanishing values at fixed points (boundary conditions are Neumann-like). They can have zero modes — constant (massless) 4D modes.
  • Odd fields must vanish at every fixed point (boundary conditions are Dirichlet-like): $\phi(y^*) = -\phi(y^*) \Rightarrow \phi(y^*) = 0$. They have no zero mode.
This is the main tool of orbifold model building: by assigning opposite parities to unwanted fields, you can project them out of the massless spectrum entirely. This is far more surgical than Higgs mechanisms or adding masses by hand.

5.1   A non-abelian example: the gauge field $A_\mu$ vs. $A_m$

In a gauge theory on $\mathbb{R}^{3,1} \times T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$, the gauge field $A_M$ has components along the four uncompact directions ($A_\mu$) and along the compact directions ($A_m$, $m=4,5$). Under $(y_4,y_5) \to (-y_4,-y_5)$, the components transform as:

$$A_\mu(-y) = +A_\mu(y) \quad \text{(4-vector is even under spatial inversion)},$$ $$A_m(-y) = -A_m(y) \quad \text{(compact-direction component flips sign)}.$$

This means $A_\mu$ has zero modes (it is the massless 4D gauge field), while $A_m$ has no zero mode — the would-be massless scalar from $A_m$ is projected out. This is the orbifold version of the Hosotani mechanism.

6. Mode Expansion and the KK Spectrum on $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$

The clearest demonstration of the orbifold projection is in the Kaluza-Klein decomposition. On the circle $S^1$ with radius $R$, a scalar field $\phi(x,y)$ has KK expansion:

$$\phi(x,y) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \phi_n(x)\,e^{iny/R}.$$

Under $y \to -y$ this becomes $\sum_n \phi_n(x) e^{-iny/R}$, so the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ condition $\phi(-y) = \pm\phi(y)$ gives $\phi_{-n} = \pm\phi_n$. Rewriting in terms of manifestly even/odd modes:

$$\phi_+(x,y) = \phi_0(x) + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \phi_n(x)\,\cos\!\left(\frac{ny}{R}\right),$$ $$\phi_-(x,y) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \phi_n(x)\,\sin\!\left(\frac{ny}{R}\right).$$

The key observation:

  • The even field $\phi_+$ has a zero mode ($n=0$): a massless 4D scalar.
  • The odd field $\phi_-$ has no zero mode: all KK modes start at mass $m_n = n/R > 0$.

This is exactly analogous to a scalar field on an interval $[0,\pi R]$ with either Neumann ($\partial_y \phi = 0$) or Dirichlet ($\phi = 0$) boundary conditions at both endpoints. The orbifold boundary conditions are the higher-dimensional analogue of Dirichlet/Neumann BCs in field theory.

QFT analogy: If you have studied the Casimir effect or field theory on an interval, you know that the choice of boundary conditions determines which modes exist. The orbifold projection is precisely a choice of boundary conditions, but now the "boundaries" are internal to the compact space.

7. Spinors and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ Action: Why There Is a Phase

So far we treated scalar fields. For spinors, the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ action is more subtle. A rotation by $2\pi$ in $SO(2)$ gives a phase $(-1)^{2s}$ for spin-$s$ fields. Since the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ inversion $(y_1,y_2) \to (-y_1,-y_2)$ is a rotation by $\pi$ in the $(y_1,y_2)$-plane, it acts on a two-component spinor $\psi = (\psi_+, \psi_-)$ as:

$$\psi(-y) = (-i)\,\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2\,P\,\psi(y),$$

where $\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2$ is the chirality matrix in the $T^2$ directions and $P = \pm 1$ is the additional parity assignment. Writing $-i\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2 = \sigma_3 = \mathrm{diag}(+1,-1)$ for the standard 2D representation, the two chirality components transform as:

$$\psi_+(-y) = +P\,\psi_+(y), \qquad \psi_-(-y) = -P\,\psi_-(y).$$

So if $P=+1$: $\psi_+$ is even and $\psi_-$ is odd. If $P=-1$: $\psi_+$ is odd and $\psi_-$ is even. In either case, the two chiralities have opposite parities — this is the mechanism that gives chiral 4D fermions from a vector-like higher-dimensional theory.

7.1   The phase $(-i)$ and its origin

The factor $-i$ comes from the spin representation of $SO(2)$. Under a rotation by angle $\alpha$ in the $(y_1,y_2)$-plane, a Dirac spinor transforms as $\psi \to e^{i\alpha\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2/2}\psi$. For $\alpha = \pi$ (a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ rotation by $\pi$):

$$e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2} = \cos\!\frac{\pi}{2} + i\sin\!\frac{\pi}{2}\,\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2 = i\tilde\Gamma^1\tilde\Gamma^2.$$

Up to the overall sign convention, this gives the factor in the boundary condition above. The point is: spinors pick up a phase under the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ even before the assignment $P$. The physical content is that you cannot assign a spinor field as purely "even" or "odd" — the two chirality components are locked into opposite parities by the spin structure. This is how the orbifold generates chirality.

Key subtlety — the integer $n$ in the paper: In arXiv:0806.4748, the full $\mathbb{Z}_2$ boundary condition for the spinor is $\psi(-y) = (-i)\tilde\Gamma^4\tilde\Gamma^5\, P\,\psi(y)$, where $P$ is an additional operator acting on gauge-space indices. For a $U(1)$ field of charge $q$, the paper writes $P = (-1)^{q+n}$ with $n$ an integer — both the charge and the free integer choice contribute. Without magnetic flux, this projection halves the zero-mode count. With magnetic flux, the projection mixes the $M$ torus modes and can yield odd-parity zero-modes not present in the pure orbifold. For what $P$ is and why it must appear, see §7.2 below.

7.2   The Gauge Embedding $P$: What It Is and Why It Must Exist

7.2.1   The problem: an orbifold identification acts on a gauge bundle

When you identify $y \sim -y$ on the torus, you are identifying points in spacetime. But a gauge theory lives on a principal bundle over spacetime — at every point there is a copy of the gauge group. When you identify two points, you must also specify how their gauge fibres are identified. This identification is an element of the gauge group: it is exactly the operator $P$.

More concretely: the orbifold boundary condition for a gauge field is not simply $A_\mu(-y) = A_\mu(y)$. In general it can be

$$A_\mu(x,-y) = P\, A_\mu(x,y)\, P^{-1},$$

where $P$ is a (possibly position-independent) gauge transformation evaluated at the fixed point. This is a large gauge transformation — it cannot be continuously deformed to the identity. Applying the orbifold twist twice must give the identity, so $P$ must satisfy

$$P^2 = \mathbf{1}.$$

This is the only constraint from consistency. The choice of $P$ is not fixed by the geometry — it is free additional data that defines the orbifold model. Different choices of $P$ give physically inequivalent theories with different spectra and different gauge groups in 4D.

Analogy from GR / geometry: In Kaluza-Klein theory, when you compactify on $S^1$ you can turn on a Wilson line $A = \theta\,dy$ — a flat gauge field with non-trivial holonomy. The orbifold projection matrix $P$ is the discrete analogue: it is the holonomy of the gauge connection around the orbifold fixed point. Because the fixed point is not a contractible loop, this holonomy is physical and cannot be gauged away.

7.2.2   The $U(1)$ case: why $P = (-1)^{q+n}$

For a $U(1)$ gauge theory, the gauge group is $\mathrm{U}(1) \ni e^{i\alpha}$. The condition $P^2 = 1$ restricts $P$ to be a square root of unity in $U(1)$: $P = e^{i\pi k} = (-1)^k$ for some integer $k$. So $P = +1$ or $P = -1$ — there are only two inequivalent choices.

A field of $U(1)$ charge $q$ transforms under the gauge transformation $P$ as

$$\psi \;\to\; P^q\,\psi = (-1)^{qk}\,\psi.$$

The paper parametrises this as $P = (-1)^{q+n}$ where $n$ is a free integer, absorbing the charge factor and the choice of $k$ into a single integer. Since $P^2 = (-1)^{2(q+n)} = 1$ automatically (because $2(q+n)$ is always even), the constraint is satisfied. The different values of $n \pmod{2}$ label the two inequivalent theories.

7.2.3   The $U(N)$ case: $P$ as a matrix in gauge space

For $U(N)$ gauge theory with the magnetic flux block-diagonal as $F_{45} = 2\pi\,\mathrm{diag}(M_1\mathbf{1}_{N_1},\ldots,M_n\mathbf{1}_{N_n})$, the unbroken gauge group is $\prod_a U(N_a)$. The orbifold identification $(y_4,y_5) \to -(y_4,y_5)$ must be embedded into this gauge group, so $P$ is now an $N\times N$ matrix satisfying $P^2 = \mathbf{1}$. Its eigenvalues are $\pm 1$, and it has the block structure

$$P = \mathrm{diag}(\eta_1\mathbf{1}_{N_1},\;\eta_2\mathbf{1}_{N_2},\;\ldots,\;\eta_n\mathbf{1}_{N_n}), \qquad \eta_a = \pm 1.$$

(The paper restricts to this block-diagonal form to preserve the unbroken $\prod_a U(N_a)$ gauge group. A general $P$ with off-diagonal structure would break the gauge group further.) The boundary conditions for the gauge field and gaugino become:

$$A_\mu(x,-y) = P\,A_\mu(x,y)\,P^{-1}, \qquad A_m(x,-y) = -P\,A_m(x,y)\,P^{-1},$$ $$\lambda_\pm(x,-y) = \pm\,P\,\lambda_\pm(x,y)\,P^{-1}.$$

7.2.4   How $P$ acts on bi-fundamental matter

This is the key to understanding the generation-counting. The bi-fundamental gaugino $\lambda^{ab}$ lives in the off-diagonal block between the $a$ and $b$ stacks. Under conjugation by $P$:

$$P\,\lambda^{ab}\,P^{-1} = \eta_a\,\lambda^{ab}\,\eta_b^{-1} = \eta_a\eta_b\,\lambda^{ab}.$$

So the orbifold boundary condition for $\lambda^{ab}_+$ becomes:

$$\lambda^{ab}_+(x,-y) = +\,\eta_a\eta_b\,\lambda^{ab}_+(x,y).$$

There are two cases:

  • $\eta_a\eta_b = +1$ (same sign): $\lambda^{ab}_+(-y) = +\lambda^{ab}_+(y)$ — the field is even. Only even-parity theta functions $\Theta^j_\mathrm{even}$ survive on each $T^2$.
  • $\eta_a\eta_b = -1$ (opposite sign): $\lambda^{ab}_+(-y) = -\lambda^{ab}_+(y)$ — the field is odd. Only odd-parity theta functions $\Theta^j_\mathrm{odd}$ survive.

The sign $\eta_a\eta_b$ is entirely determined by the relative sign between the $a$- and $b$-blocks in $P$. Since the two chiralities $\lambda^{ab}_\pm$ have opposite boundary conditions (from the $\pm$ in $\lambda_\pm \to \pm P\lambda_\pm P^{-1}$), only one chirality will have even-parity wave functions and the other will have odd-parity ones. This is the mechanism that selects which theta functions survive and thus determines the generation count.

Summary of the role of $P$:
  • $P$ is the gauge embedding of the orbifold twist — it specifies how the geometric identification acts on the gauge bundle.
  • It is free data: $P^2 = \mathbf{1}$ is the only constraint. Different choices give different physical theories.
  • For $U(1)$: $P = \pm 1$ (two choices, labelled by integer $n$ in the paper via $P = (-1)^{q+n}$).
  • For $U(N)$: $P$ is a diagonal $\pm 1$ matrix. The relative signs $\eta_a\eta_b$ determine whether bi-fundamental fields are even or odd, hence which theta functions survive the projection.
  • The full boundary condition $\psi(-y) = (-i)\tilde\Gamma^4\tilde\Gamma^5\,P\,\psi(y)$ factorises into two independent pieces: the geometric factor $(-i)\tilde\Gamma^4\tilde\Gamma^5$ (acting on spinor indices, fixed by the spin representation) and the gauge factor $P$ (acting on gauge indices, a free choice).

7.2.5   Explicit example from arXiv:0806.4748 (Model 1)

For the Pati-Salam model with $N_a=4, N_b=2, N_c=2$, the paper chooses

$$P = P' = \mathrm{diag}(\underbrace{+1,\ldots,+1}_{N_a=4},\; \underbrace{-1,\ldots,-1}_{N_b=2},\; \underbrace{+1,\ldots,+1}_{N_c=2}).$$

The relative signs are: $\eta_a = +1$, $\eta_b = -1$, $\eta_c = +1$. Therefore:

  • $\eta_a\eta_b = -1$: $\lambda^{ab}$ is odd under the orbifold — only $\Theta^j_\mathrm{odd}$ survive on each projected $T^2$.
  • $\eta_c\eta_a = +1$: $\lambda^{ca}$ is even — only $\Theta^j_\mathrm{even}$ survive.
  • $\eta_b\eta_c = -1$: $\lambda^{bc}$ (Higgs) is odd.

Combined with the flux quantum numbers $(I^1_{ab}, I^2_{ab}, I^3_{ab}) = (3,4,1)$, the zero-mode count after projection follows the even/odd table: flux $3$ has 1 odd mode, flux $4$ has 2 even modes, flux $1$ has 0 odd modes. Since $\lambda^{ab}$ is odd, the surviving count per $T^2$ is $(1,\,I^2_{ab(\mathrm{even})},\,0)$. Wait — but $\lambda^{ab}$ is odd on each $T^2$ separately only when $P$ acts on that $T^2$. For Model 1 with $P = P' = \mathrm{diag}(\mathbf{1}_4,-\mathbf{1}_2,\mathbf{1}_2)$, the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ acts on $T^2_{(1)}$ and $T^2_{(2)}$ simultaneously (see §10.1). The $Z_2$ boundary condition involves the product of chiralities $s_1 s_2$: $\lambda^{ab}_{s_1,s_2,s_3}(-y_{1,2}) = s_1 s_2\,\eta_a\eta_b\,\lambda^{ab}_{s_1,s_2,s_3}(y)$. For the zero-mode sector $s_1=s_2=+1$, this gives eigenvalue $\eta_a\eta_b = -1$, so the surviving modes need $\Theta^{j_1}_\mathrm{odd}(T^2_{(1)})\,\Theta^{j_2}_\mathrm{odd}(T^2_{(2)})$ or $\Theta^{j_1}_\mathrm{even}\,\Theta^{j_2}_\mathrm{even}$. After applying both $Z_2$ and $Z'_2$ (see §10), only one combination is consistent. The net result: $1\times 3\times 1 = 3$ families (as shown in the companion explanation file).

8. Why Orbifolds Break Supersymmetry

Orbifolds are one of the most efficient tools for breaking extended supersymmetry. The logic is straightforward and follows directly from the projection:

Consider starting from 10D $\mathcal{N}=1$ super Yang-Mills, compactified on $T^6$. The 4D theory has $\mathcal{N}=4$ supersymmetry — far too much for realistic model building. The supercharges transform as spinors under the $SO(6) \cong SU(4)$ rotation group of $T^6$. Under the $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}'_2$ orbifold action (two $\mathbb{Z}_2$ rotations in different planes), only a subset of supercharges is even under the projection.

Concretely, the four complex supercharges $Q_\alpha$ of the $\mathcal{N}=4$ theory decompose under the orbifold group. Each $\mathbb{Z}_2$ acting on two of the three $T^2$ factors flips the sign of two of the four supercharges. Only supercharges that are even under all elements of the orbifold group survive. For $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}'_2$:

$Q_2$$-1$$-1$Yes
$Q_3$$-1$$+1$No
$Q_4$$+1$$-1$No

Schematic: the $\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2$ orbifold retains 2 of 4 supercharges, breaking $\mathcal{N}=4 \to \mathcal{N}=2$ (or to $\mathcal{N}=1$ with further conditions).

Precisely: the $T^6/(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2)$ orbifold in arXiv:0806.4748 preserves $\mathcal{N}=1$ in 4D — it breaks the original $\mathcal{N}=4$ by a factor of 4. This is one of the two most common orbifold compactifications for realistic model building (the other being $T^6/\mathbb{Z}_3$, which also preserves $\mathcal{N}=1$).

The SUSY-breaking mechanism in brief: The supercharges $Q$ are spinors in the compact dimensions. The orbifold group acts on them, and only supercharges that are invariant (i.e., even under the action) survive to 4D as symmetries. The orbifold is like a "projection operator" on the SUSY algebra — it is discrete, exact, and under full analytic control.

9. Twisted Sectors in String Theory

Everything above applies to field theory on orbifold backgrounds. In string theory, there is an entirely new ingredient with no field-theory counterpart: the twisted sectors.

9.1   Closed strings on orbifolds

A closed string satisfies the periodicity condition $X^\mu(\sigma + 2\pi) = X^\mu(\sigma)$ (up to winding on the torus). On the orbifold $M/G$, we must allow twisted boundary conditions: the string can close up to a group element $g \in G$,

$$X^m(\sigma + 2\pi) = g \cdot X^m(\sigma),$$

because the two ends of the closed string are identified under $g$ in the orbifold. For each group element $g \in G$ (including $g=e$, the untwisted sector), there is a distinct sector of the string Hilbert space.

9.2   The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ twisted sector: a simple example

On $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$ with the action $y \to -y$, the twisted sector has the boundary condition

$$X(\sigma + 2\pi) = -X(\sigma).$$

Expanding in modes, this requires half-integer moding:

$$X(\sigma,\tau) = x_0 + \sum_{r \in \mathbb{Z}+\frac{1}{2}} \left(\alpha_r e^{-ir(\tau-\sigma)} + \bar\alpha_r e^{-ir(\tau+\sigma)}\right).$$

The key point: the twisted sector has no zero mode $x_0$ — the string is stuck at a fixed point. The ground state of the twisted sector is a particle localized at the orbifold fixed point.

Twisted sector = new particles localized at singularities: The orbifold fixed points appear as singular conical singularities from the spacetime perspective. But string theory automatically "fills in" these singularities: the twisted sector states are new massless (or light) particles living exactly at the fixed points. They are not visible in the low-energy field theory of the untwisted sector — they are intrinsically stringy states that appear because strings can wrap around the cone tip.

9.3   The orbifold projection on closed strings

To form a consistent theory on the orbifold, one also imposes the orbifold projection on the Hilbert space: only states invariant under $G$ are kept. In the untwisted sector, this discards states odd under $G$ (as in field theory). In each twisted sector, this further selects a subset of twisted states. The full orbifold string theory is:

$$\mathcal{H}_{\text{orbifold}} = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g,h\in G} \mathcal{H}_{g,h},$$

where $\mathcal{H}_{g,h}$ denotes the sector of strings with $g$-twisted boundary conditions projected onto the $h$-invariant subspace (modular invariance requires summing over both).

9.4   Why twisted sectors are needed for consistency

The orbifold projection alone (dropping the twisted sectors) gives a modular-non-invariant theory. Modular invariance of the partition function — the string-theory analogue of UV finiteness and anomaly cancellation — requires the twisted sectors to be present. Including them, the partition function automatically satisfies:

$$Z\!\left(\tau+1\right) = Z(\tau), \qquad Z\!\left(-1/\tau\right) = Z(\tau),$$

which are the two generators of modular transformations $SL(2,\mathbb{Z})$ acting on the torus worldsheet. This is a deep consistency condition of string theory with no direct analogue in field theory.

10. The Full Orbifold $T^6/(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2)$ in Model Building

Let us now put everything together and describe the orbifold used in arXiv:0806.4748. The setup is $\mathcal{N}=1$ SYM in 10D (from the open-string sector of a type IIB or type I theory, or a heterotic theory), compactified on $T^6/(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2)$.

10.1   The two $\mathbb{Z}_2$ actions

Label the coordinates of $T^6 = T^2_{(1)} \times T^2_{(2)} \times T^2_{(3)}$ as $(y_4,y_5)$, $(y_6,y_7)$, $(y_8,y_9)$. The two group elements act as:

$$\mathbb{Z}_2:\quad (y_4,y_5) \to -(y_4,y_5),\quad (y_6,y_7) \to -(y_6,y_7),\quad (y_8,y_9) \to +(y_8,y_9),$$ $$\mathbb{Z}'_2:\quad (y_4,y_5) \to -(y_4,y_5),\quad (y_6,y_7) \to +(y_6,y_7),\quad (y_8,y_9) \to -(y_8,y_9).$$

Their product $\mathbb{Z}_2 \cdot \mathbb{Z}'_2$ acts as $(y_4,y_5) \to +(y_4,y_5)$, $(y_6,y_7) \to -(y_6,y_7)$, $(y_8,y_9) \to -(y_8,y_9)$. Thus the full orbifold group $G = \{e,\,\theta,\,\theta',\,\theta\theta'\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}'_2$ acts simultaneously on all three $T^2$ factors.

10.2   Fixed loci and twisted sectors

For each non-trivial group element, the fixed locus is a product of fixed sets on each $T^2$:

  • Under $\theta$: fixed on $T^2_{(1)}$ and $T^2_{(2)}$ (4 points each), free on $T^2_{(3)}$. Fixed locus: $4\times 4 = 16$ fixed points $\times T^2_{(3)}$ = 16 two-tori.
  • Under $\theta'$: analogously, 16 two-tori.
  • Under $\theta\theta'$: 16 two-tori (on $T^2_{(2)}\times T^2_{(3)}$, free on $T^2_{(1)}$).

Each fixed torus carries twisted-sector states — these are D6-branes (in the T-dual intersecting brane picture) or lower-dimensional brane modes in the direct magnetized-orbifold description. In arXiv:0806.4748, these are called brane modes: they are localized fields that live on the orbifold fixed loci and can only couple to bulk zero-modes with even wave functions (since odd profiles vanish at the fixed points).

10.3   The combined effect: generation counting

On the pure torus $T^6$, the bi-fundamental matter between D-brane stacks $a$ and $b$ has $I_{ab} = \prod_{i=1}^3 (M^{(i)}_a - M^{(i)}_b)$ zero-modes (families). After the $\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2$ orbifold:

  • Each $T^2_{(i)}$ contributes either even or odd zero-modes, depending on the projection. On $T^2_{(i)}$ alone, the $|M^{(i)}|$ torus zero-modes split into $\lceil |M^{(i)}|/2 \rceil$ even modes and $\lfloor |M^{(i)}|/2 \rfloor$ odd modes.
  • Consistency of the two $\mathbb{Z}_2$ projections requires the surviving modes to be either all-even or all-odd products across the three $T^2$ factors.
  • The total family count is therefore much smaller than $|I_{ab}|$, and can be as low as 3.
Why this is powerful: On the torus alone, achieving exactly 3 families requires $\prod_i |M^{(i)}_a - M^{(i)}_b| = 3$, which severely constrains the flux choices. On the orbifold, one can start with, say, $|I_{ab}| = 12$ families on the torus and have the orbifold reduce this to 3 by projecting out 3/4 of the modes. This gives far more freedom in satisfying supersymmetry conditions and tadpole cancellation, while still achieving the observed three-generation structure.

11. Summary: The Orbifold as a Tool

  1. Definition: An orbifold $M/G$ is the quotient of a smooth manifold $M$ by a discrete group $G$ acting by isometries. Fixed points (where some $g\in G$ acts trivially) are conical singularities.
  2. Boundary conditions for fields: Fields on $M/G$ must transform in representations of $G$. For $\mathbb{Z}_2$: fields are either even ($P=+1$) or odd ($P=-1$). Odd fields vanish at fixed points and have no zero mode.
  3. KK reduction: On $S^1/\mathbb{Z}_2$: even KK modes are $\cos(ny/R)$ (including the $n=0$ zero mode), odd modes are $\sin(ny/R)$ (no zero mode). The zero-mode spectrum is halved compared to $S^1$.
  4. Spinors: The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ rotation by $\pi$ in a 2-plane acts on spinors with an extra sign from the spin representation: the two chirality components have opposite parities. This is the mechanism generating 4D chirality from a vector-like higher-dimensional theory.
  5. SUSY breaking: Supercharges are spinors in the compact space. The orbifold projects out those with the wrong parity, reducing $\mathcal{N}=4 \to \mathcal{N}=1$ for $T^6/(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}'_2)$.
  6. Twisted sectors (string theory): Strings can wind around orbifold fixed points with twisted boundary conditions. These new states are localized at the fixed points, are required for modular invariance, and resolve the conical singularities of the orbifold. They have no field-theory analogue.
  7. Model building: The orbifold projection reduces the generation number, projects out unwanted massless fields, breaks extended supersymmetry, and can enforce texture zeroes in Yukawa matrices — all in a geometrically transparent and exactly controllable way.
