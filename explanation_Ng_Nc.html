Why the Non-Invertible Symmetry Requires $N_g = N_c = 3$

Context. This is an explanation of a key claim in Non-Invertible Peccei-Quinn Symmetry and the Massless Quark Solution to the Strong CP Problem (Córdova, Hong, Koren; arXiv:2402.12453). The authors construct a non-invertible Peccei-Quinn symmetry that protects $\bar\theta = 0$, and state that this symmetry exists only because the Standard Model has $N_g = N_c = 3$ (3 generations = 3 colors). The argument is a chain of three steps.
Step 1 — A special $\mathbb{Z}_3$ identification exists in the SM

The authors consider extending the SM by a gauged horizontal baryon-number symmetry

$$U(1)_H \;=\; B_1 + B_2 - 2B_3,$$

where $B_i$ is the baryon number of generation $i$. A crucial observation is that, with the SM matter content, four completely independent $\mathbb{Z}_3$ subgroups — one from $SU(3)_C$, one from $U(1)_Y$, one from $U(1)_B$, and one from $U(1)_H$ — all act identically on every field:

$$\left[\mathbb{Z}_3^{H} \subset U(1)_H\right] \;=\; \left[\mathbb{Z}_3^{C} \subset SU(3)_C\right] \;=\; \left[\mathbb{Z}_3^{Y} \subset U(1)_Y\right] \;=\; \left[\mathbb{Z}_3^{B} \subset U(1)_B\right].$$

Why does this need $N_g = N_c$? The $\mathbb{Z}_3$ of baryon number acts on quarks as a phase $e^{2\pi i/3}$ (since each quark carries baryon number $1/3$ and there are 3 of them per baryon). This is identical to the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ center of $SU(3)_C$ acting on the fundamental representation. This coincidence — that the number of colors equals the number of quarks per baryon, which in turn equals the number of generations — is precisely $N_c = N_g = 3$. For any other values, these $\mathbb{Z}_3$ subgroups would not be isomorphic as symmetry actions, and the identification would fail.

Step 2 — The identification permits a non-trivial gauge group quotient

Because the diagonal $\mathbb{Z}_3$ above acts trivially on all matter fields, one is free to quotient the gauge group by it without changing the physics of any local operator. The relevant choice is:

$$SU(3)_C \times U(1)_H \quad\longrightarrow\quad \frac{SU(3)_C \times U(1)_H}{\mathbb{Z}_3}.$$

This quotient has two physical consequences:

  • It removes Wilson lines in certain representations from the spectrum (those charged under the quotiented $\mathbb{Z}_3$).
  • By Dirac quantization, it admits new magnetic monopoles carrying $\mathbb{Z}_3$ magnetic charge, corresponding to a new $\mathbb{Z}_3^{(1)}$ magnetic one-form symmetry. These monopoles are the source of fractional instantons — gauge field configurations with instanton number valued in $\frac{1}{3}\mathbb{Z}$ rather than integers.

Concretely, the $SU(3)_C$ instanton number $\mathcal{N}_C$ can now be fractional, with its fractional part controlled by the discrete magnetic flux $\omega(A_C) \in H^2(M, \mathbb{Z}_3)$:

$$\mathcal{N}_C \;=\; \frac{1}{8\pi^2}\int_M \mathrm{Tr}(F_C \wedge F_C) \;=\; \frac{1}{3}\int_M \omega \wedge \omega \quad \bmod 1.$$

Furthermore, because the quotient acts simultaneously on both $SU(3)_C$ and $U(1)_H$, the fractional instanton numbers of the two gauge factors are correlated:

$$\mathcal{N}_C \;=\; 6\,\mathcal{N}_H \quad \bmod 1,$$

where $\mathcal{N}_H = \frac{1}{8\pi^2}\int_M F_H \wedge F_H$. The minimal fractional instanton therefore has $\mathcal{N}_H = 1/9$ and $\mathcal{N}_C = 2/3$. This entire option is only available because of the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ identification established in Step 1, which in turn requires $N_g = N_c$.

Step 3 — Fractional instantons promote an invertible symmetry to a non-invertible one

With vanishing down-type Yukawa couplings $y_d \to 0$, the SM possesses classical flavor symmetries. The SM-anomaly-free combinations relevant here are:

$$A_1 = \bar{d}_3 - \bar{d}_1,\quad A_2 = \bar{d}_3 - \bar{d}_2,\quad A_3 = \tilde{B}_3 - \tilde{B}_1,\quad A_4 = \tilde{B}_3 - \tilde{B}_2,$$

where $\tilde{B}_i = Q_i - \bar{u}_i$.

Without the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ quotient (ordinary instantons only):

The ABJ anomaly coefficients of each $U(1)_{A_i}$ with standard, integer-quantized instantons are:

$$\left[U(1)_{A_i}\right]\left[U(1)_H\right]^2 = 3N_c.$$

A GCD analysis shows each $U(1)_{A_i}$ is broken down to a $\mathbb{Z}_{3N_c}$ invertible discrete symmetry. The down Yukawa is merely a spurion of an ordinary symmetry — nothing prevents it from being nonzero, and $\bar\theta$ is not protected in any deep way.

With the $\left(SU(3)_C \times U(1)_H\right)/\mathbb{Z}_3$ quotient (fractional instantons included):

In the background of the minimal fractional instanton ($\mathcal{N}_H = 1/9$, $\mathcal{N}_C = 2/3$), the anomaly coefficient for each $U(1)_{A_i}$ from the color–horizontal (CH) instanton background is:

$$\left[U(1)_{A_i}\right][\text{CH}] \;=\; 1.$$

The anomaly coefficient is now 1 — the GCD of all contributions is 1 — so the fractional instantons break $U(1)_{A_i}$ completely. In particular, a discrete $\mathbb{Z}_3$ subgroup

$$\mathbb{Z}_3^{\tilde{B}+\bar{d}}$$

which acts generation-independently, is a non-invertible symmetry. Under this symmetry, all $U(1)_H$-allowed entries of the down Yukawa matrix are forbidden:

$$\mathcal{L}_{y_d} \;=\; y_d^{ij}\, H\, Q_i\, \bar{d}_j \;=\; 0 \quad \text{enforced by } \mathbb{Z}_3^{\tilde{B}+\bar{d}}.$$

Why is this non-invertible rather than just broken?

The fractional instantons that would break $U(1)_{A_i}$ have instanton number $\mathcal{N}_C = 2/3 \notin \mathbb{Z}$. On a topologically trivial spacetime $\mathbb{R}^4$ (or its IR regulator $S^4$), non-abelian instanton numbers are always integers — so these configurations cannot exist on flat space. They only become available in topologically non-trivial backgrounds, i.e., in the presence of magnetic charges ('t Hooft lines).

This is the defining feature of a non-invertible symmetry: the symmetry defect operator implementing a finite $\mathbb{Z}_3$ rotation cannot simply be $\mathcal{D} \times \overline{\mathcal{D}} = 1$; instead it obeys the fusion rule

$$\mathcal{D}(\Sigma) \times \overline{\mathcal{D}}(\Sigma) \;\sim\; \sum_{\text{two-cycles } S \subset \Sigma} \exp\!\left(\frac{i}{16\pi N}\int_S F_{\alpha\beta}\, dS_{\mu\nu}\,\varepsilon^{\alpha\beta\mu\nu}\right),$$

leaving behind a condensate of magnetic one-form symmetry operators. The symmetry acts correctly on local operators (as a $\mathbb{Z}_3$ selection rule), but fails to be represented by unitary operators on the full Hilbert space — the hallmark of non-invertibility. As a consequence, when $\mathbb{Z}_3^{\tilde{B}+\bar{d}}$ is preserved, the strong CP phase satisfies

$$\mathrm{Im}(M) \;=\; \mathrm{Im}\!\left(e^{i\theta}\det(y_u\, y_d)\right) \;=\; 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \bar\theta = 0.$$
The Punchline

The entire argument is a single chain of logical dependencies:

$$\underbrace{N_g = N_c = 3}_{\text{SM accident}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\mathbb{Z}_3^C = \mathbb{Z}_3^H}_{\text{Step 1: identification}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\frac{SU(3)_C \times U(1)_H}{\mathbb{Z}_3}\text{ consistent}}_{\text{Step 2: quotient allowed}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\mathcal{N}_C \in \tfrac{1}{3}\mathbb{Z}}_{\text{fractional instantons exist}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\left[U(1)_{A_i}\right][\text{CH}]=1}_{\text{Step 3: anomaly coeff.} = 1} \;\Longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\mathbb{Z}_3^{\tilde{B}+\bar{d}}\text{ non-invertible}}_{\text{non-invertible PQ symmetry}} \;\Longrightarrow\; \bar\theta = 0.$$

Every arrow in this chain breaks if $N_g \neq N_c$. The non-invertible Peccei-Quinn symmetry, and the massless-quark solution to the strong CP problem it enables, are therefore an accidental consequence of the SM having the same number of generations as colors — a numerological coincidence of the SM promoted to a structural symmetry principle.
