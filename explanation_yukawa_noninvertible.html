Yukawa Textures from Non-Invertible Symmetries

T. Kobayashi, H. Otsuka, M. Tanimoto — arXiv:2409.05270
Summary and connection to magnetized orbifold models (arXiv:0806.4748)

1. What the Paper Does

The paper applies non-invertible symmetries to the flavor problem of quarks and leptons. The core idea is simple:

  1. Start with a $\mathbb{Z}_M$ discrete symmetry among fermion generations.
  2. Gauge a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ subgroup (i.e., identify charges $q \sim M-q$).
  3. The resulting symmetry is non-invertible: a single state carries both charge $k$ and charge $M-k$ simultaneously, so the "fusion" of representations is not group-like.

The authors then systematically classify which Yukawa textures (patterns of zeros in the mass matrix) this non-invertible symmetry can enforce, for $M = 3, 4, 5, 6, 7$, and show that some of these textures are impossible to obtain from any conventional (invertible) symmetry.

2. The Non-Invertible Symmetry: Construction

The $\mathbb{Z}_M$ generator $g$ has $M$ conjugacy classes $g^0, g^1, \ldots, g^{M-1}$. Introduce the outer automorphism $r: g \mapsto g^{-1}$ and gauge the $\mathbb{Z}_2 = \{e, r\}$. This identifies $g^k$ with $g^{M-k}$, giving equivalence classes:

$$[g^k] = \{g^k,\, g^{M-k}\}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor M/2 \rfloor.$$

The number of distinct classes is $\lfloor M/2 \rfloor + 1$. The fusion rule for these classes is:

$$[g^k]\,[g^{k'}] = [g^{k+k'}] + [g^{M-k+k'}].$$

This is not a group multiplication — the product of two simple objects gives a sum of two objects. This is the hallmark of a non-invertible (categorical) symmetry.

Selection rule: An $n$-point coupling among fields in classes $[g^{k_1}], \ldots, [g^{k_n}]$ is allowed if and only if there exists a choice of signs such that $$\sum_{i=1}^n \pm k_i \equiv 0 \pmod{M}.$$ Intuitively, each field simultaneously carries charges $+k$ and $-k$ (mod $M$), and you can pick either sign for each field independently.

3. Key Example: $M=5$ and the NNI Texture

For $M = 5$, there are 3 classes: $[g^0]$, $[g^1]$, $[g^2]$ — perfect for three generations. Assign the three generations of left-handed and right-handed fermions to these classes. If the Higgs is in representation $[g^1]$, the Yukawa matrix is:

$$Y_{[g^1]} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & a & 0 \\ b & 0 & c \\ 0 & d & e \end{pmatrix}.$$

This is the nearest neighbor interaction (NNI) texture — a phenomenologically important form that is known to be consistent with all observed CKM mixing and quark masses. The NNI texture cannot be derived from any conventional $U(1)$ or $\mathbb{Z}_N$ symmetry without introducing extra particles (e.g., multiple Higgs fields). The non-invertible symmetry produces it naturally.

Why conventional symmetry fails for NNI: Suppose two left-handed quarks have $U(1)$ charges $Q_1, Q_2$ and two right-handed quarks have $q_1, q_2$. Non-vanishing entries at positions $(1,2)$, $(2,1)$, and $(2,2)$ require $Q_1 + q_2 = Q_2 + q_1 = Q_2 + q_2$, implying $Q_1 = Q_2$ and $q_1 = q_2$. But then the $(1,1)$ entry should also be allowed — contradicting the texture zero. No assignment of $U(1)$ or $\mathbb{Z}_N$ charges can enforce this pattern.

4. Phenomenological Applications

4.1 Quarks

In a supersymmetric setup with two Higgs doublets $H_U$ and $H_D$ carrying different non-invertible charges, the NNI basis is obtained for both up- and down-type quarks with $M=5$, $H_{U,D}$ in class $[g^1]$. This is fully consistent with the CKM matrix and quark masses.

Additionally, the "Occam's Razor" textures of Ref. [Tanimoto 2016] — minimal-parameter down-quark mass matrices in the diagonal-up basis — can also be obtained by suitable charge assignments.

4.2 Leptons

The seesaw mechanism texture of Ref. [Kaneta–Tanimoto 2016], where:

  • The charged lepton Yukawa $Y_E$ is diagonal ($H_D \in [g^0]$),
  • The Dirac neutrino Yukawa $Y_{\nu D}$ has NNI form ($H_U \in [g^1]$),
  • The right-handed Majorana mass $M_R$ is diagonal (guaranteed by the non-invertible symmetry),

is reproduced. This texture is consistent with observed neutrino oscillation data (normal hierarchy).

5. Connection to Zero Modes of Magnetized Orbifold Models

This is where the paper's construction meets string theory. The non-invertible symmetry studied here is not just a bottom-up invention — it arises naturally in magnetized D-brane models on $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ orbifolds, as shown in Ref. [Kobayashi–Otsuka–Tanimoto 2024] and rooted in the magnetized orbifold framework of Ref. [Abe–Kobayashi–Ohki 2008].

5.1 The $\mathbb{Z}_M$ symmetry from the magnetized torus

On $T^2$ with magnetic flux $M$, there are $|M|$ chiral zero-modes with wave functions

$$\Theta^j(y) \propto e^{-M\pi y_4^2}\,\vartheta\!\left[\begin{smallmatrix} j/M \\ 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]\!(M z, M\tau), \qquad j = 0, 1, \ldots, M-1.$$

These $M$ zero-modes are quasi-localized at different positions on $T^2$. They carry a $\mathbb{Z}_M$ charge $j$ originating from the discrete translation symmetry of the torus: a shift by $1/M$ of a lattice period maps $\Theta^j \to e^{2\pi i j/M}\,\Theta^j$. The Yukawa selection rule on the torus is simply $\mathbb{Z}_M$ charge conservation:

$$j_1 + j_2 + j_3 \equiv 0 \pmod{M}.$$

5.2 Orbifolding: $\mathbb{Z}_2$ identification $\Rightarrow$ non-invertible symmetry

Now orbifold: $T^2 \to T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$, identifying $y \sim -y$. The $\mathbb{Z}_2$ acts on zero-modes as $\Theta^j(-y) = \Theta^{M-j}(y)$, so it identifies mode $j$ with mode $M-j$. The surviving zero-modes on the orbifold are the $\mathbb{Z}_2$-projected combinations:

$$\Theta^j_{\rm even} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\Theta^j + \Theta^{M-j}\bigr), \qquad \Theta^j_{\rm odd} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\Theta^j - \Theta^{M-j}\bigr).$$

The number of surviving zero-modes on the orbifold is $\lfloor M/2 \rfloor + 1$ (for one parity sector) — exactly the number of classes $[g^k]$ of the non-invertible symmetry.

5.3 Why the orbifold selection rule is non-invertible

On the torus, each zero-mode $\Theta^j$ has a single well-defined $\mathbb{Z}_M$ charge $j$. This is an ordinary (invertible) symmetry.

On the orbifold, the physical zero-mode $\Theta^j_{\rm even} = (\Theta^j + \Theta^{M-j})/\sqrt{2}$ is a superposition of the original modes with charges $j$ and $M-j$. It does not have a definite charge under $\mathbb{Z}_M$ — it carries both charges simultaneously. When computing the Yukawa overlap integral, either "branch" ($j$ or $M-j$) can contribute, so the coupling is non-zero whenever any sign choice gives $\sum \pm k_i \equiv 0 \pmod{M}$.

For $M$ odd, no residual invertible symmetry survives — the selection rule is purely non-invertible. For $M$ even, only a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ invertible part survives (even $k$ vs. odd $k$), but the full selection rule is still more restrictive than $\mathbb{Z}_2$.

5.4 Concrete example: $M=5$ on $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$

Flux $M = 5$ gives 5 torus zero-modes: $\Theta^0, \Theta^1, \Theta^2, \Theta^3, \Theta^4$. Under $\mathbb{Z}_2$: $\Theta^0$ is self-identified, $\Theta^1 \leftrightarrow \Theta^4$, $\Theta^2 \leftrightarrow \Theta^3$. The orbifold zero-modes (even parity) are:

$$\Theta^0_{\rm even} \longleftrightarrow [g^0], \qquad \frac{\Theta^1 + \Theta^4}{\sqrt{2}} \longleftrightarrow [g^1], \qquad \frac{\Theta^2 + \Theta^3}{\sqrt{2}} \longleftrightarrow [g^2].$$

These are exactly 3 zero-modes — perfect for three generations. The Yukawa coupling for a Higgs field in class $[g^1]$ is non-zero if and only if $\pm k_L \pm k_R \pm 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{5}$, which gives precisely the NNI texture.

In the magnetized orbifold language, this texture arises because the overlap integrals $\int \Theta^{k_L}_{\rm even}\,\Theta^{k_R}_{\rm even}\,\Theta^{1}_{\rm even}$ vanish for certain combinations by the $\mathbb{Z}_M$ selection rule acting on the constituent torus modes, and survive for others where one of the two "branches" ($j$ or $M-j$) can make the charges add up.

6. The Big Picture

Top-down meets bottom-up:
  • Top-down (string theory): Magnetized D-branes on $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ naturally produce zero-modes whose Yukawa couplings obey a non-invertible selection rule. The $\mathbb{Z}_M$ from the torus translation is broken by the orbifold to something that is not a group but a fusion category.
  • Bottom-up (this paper): Abstract the non-invertible selection rule away from the string construction and use it as a model-building tool. The result: Yukawa textures like NNI that no conventional symmetry can enforce, applied successfully to quarks and leptons.

The connection is not just an analogy. The paper explicitly states (Section 2, footnotes) that the $\mathbb{Z}_M$ is the discrete translation on the torus, and the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ gauging corresponds to the $T^2/\mathbb{Z}_2$ orbifold. The number of non-invertible representations equals the number of orbifold zero-modes. The coupling selection rule is the same.
